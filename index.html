Why it matters now

The cybersecurity skills gap is a measurement problem.

Not a training problem. Not a tooling problem. You cannot hire what you cannot measure — and certifications don't measure reasoning.

Hiring is slow and inconsistent

Security teams typically run 3–5 technical rounds per candidate. Same person, different outcomes depending on who interviewed. Senior engineer time burns either way.

Certifications test recall, not reasoning

Real incidents demand judgment under uncertainty — something a multiple-choice exam cannot measure. A CISSP tells you someone studied. It doesn't tell you how they'd triage at 2am.

Attack surface keeps expanding

Cloud, SaaS, APIs, supply chains. Your team needs to reason through novel attack paths every week — not recite frameworks. Practical reasoning is the differentiator.

Transparent scoring

Five anchored dimensions. Zero black boxes.

Every answer is evaluated by our 3-Pass AI engine — Evaluator, Challenger, Reconciler — against a published rubric with concrete written anchors at every score band. Candidates see exactly how they were evaluated. Hiring managers get defensible, explainable scores.

Dimension 1

Threat Identification

Did the candidate correctly identify attack vectors, techniques, and threat actor behavior? At 9–10: precise MITRE technique IDs, real-world parallels, false positives ruled out.

Dimension 2

Containment & Response

Effective containment strategy that minimizes blast radius while preserving evidence. At 9–10: operational depth, second-order effects, compliance timelines (CERT-In, GDPR, DPDPA).

Dimension 3

Architecture & Blast Radius

Understanding of architectural interconnections and accurate impact assessment. At 9–10: third-party impact, identifies architectural weaknesses, proposes segmentation improvements.

Dimension 4

Communication Quality

Clarity of reasoning, structural organization, audience-appropriateness. At 9–10: adapts language to audience, effective in board briefing or technical deep-dive.

Dimension 5

Framework Application

Correct reference to and application of MITRE ATT&CK, NIST, CIS, ISO 27001. At 9–10: seamlessly integrates multiple frameworks, knows when they apply and when they don't.

Each question weights these dimensions differently — a "map this to MITRE" question weights Threat Identification at 40%; an "executive briefing" question weights Communication at 50%. The full weighting matrix is in our published rubric.

FAQ

Questions we hear.

How is this different from Hack The Box, TryHackMe, or a CTF?

Hack The Box and TryHackMe teach hands-on exploitation through long-form labs and CTF challenges. Both are excellent learning platforms.

ThreatReady solves a different problem: can a candidate reason through an attack chain in 30 minutes, the way a senior interviewer would ask them to? Adaptive AI questions, MITRE ATT&CK mapped architectures, and a transparent rubric. It's not a replacement for learning — it's a replacement for technical phone screens.

What's new in the 15 tracks — IAM, Data, AI/LLM Security?

IAM Security covers identity governance, federated access patterns, and the privilege-escalation reasoning that catches real cloud breaches. Scenarios include OAuth consent grant attacks, cross-tenant access, and IAM Roles Anywhere misuse.

Data Security covers data classification, encryption strategy, DLP design, and data-residency reasoning. Scenarios include S3 bucket exfiltration, GDPR/DPDPA-compliant pipelines, and cross-region replication risks.

AI/LLM Security covers prompt injection, output validation, model supply-chain risks, and the unique threat surface of LLM-integrated applications. Scenarios include agentic-tool attacks, jailbreak chains, and training-data poisoning detection.

What is the Career Genome and how is it different from a certification?

Certifications are binary — you have CISSP or you don't. They expire. They test memorized knowledge.

The Career Genome is a continuously-updated, AI-evaluated, fraud-resistant skills profile mapped to NICE Workforce Framework and MITRE ATT&CK. It shows exactly where you stand across dozens of sub-skills with verifiable session evidence — not self-reported claims. Share the public verification link on LinkedIn or attach to applications. It updates every session and never goes stale.

How are scenarios kept current?

Our scenario architecture pool is reviewed and updated regularly to reflect current attack patterns. New scenarios are added based on real-world incidents — public breach reports, MITRE ATT&CK technique additions, CISA Known Exploited Vulnerabilities entries, and NVD critical CVEs.

Each difficulty level has multiple architectures from a global pool, and on retake the scoring engine selects a different architecture so memorisation doesn't help. Scenarios that become outdated are retired. The goal is that what you face on ThreatReady looks like what your team will actually triage next quarter — not a 2018 playbook.

How does adaptive questioning work mid-session?

After each answer, the AI generates the next question based on what you actually said — not a pre-written question list. Strong answers earn harder follow-ups; shallow answers trigger clarifying ones. This serves two purposes: it prevents pre-prepared answers from gaming the assessment, and it explores depth where you've shown competence.

Adaptive questions are bounded by the scenario's MITRE ATT&CK tactics and the role's competency map — the AI cannot wander into unrelated domains. Combined with question variants on retake, this is the anti-gaming mechanism that lets one ThreatReady score replace 2–3 interview rounds.

How does role-readiness scoring work?

Once you've completed assessments across multiple roles and modules, ThreatReady computes role-readiness percentages for target roles. Each target role has a competency profile that maps to the five anchored dimensions — for example, Senior Cloud Security Engineer might require Communication Quality ≥ 7.5, Architecture & Blast Radius ≥ 7.0, Containment & Response ≥ 7.5.

Your dimension scores are weighted against the profile to produce a readiness percentage. The system surfaces your weakest dimension and recommends the modules that will close the gap fastest. Readiness updates after every session, so it's always current.

How does the 3-Pass scoring engine actually work?

Single-pass AI scoring is easy to fool. Verbose-but-shallow answers score high. Hallucinated CVE numbers go undetected. Fluent-but-wrong responses pass through.

Our scoring engine runs every answer through three independent AI passes:

Pass 1 — Evaluator: scores generously against the anchored rubric, citing specific evidence from your answer. Tends to over-credit verbose responses (intentionally — Pass 2 corrects).

Pass 2 — Challenger: adversarially reviews. Catches technical errors, fake CVEs, non-existent MITRE techniques, fabricated tools, inflated Pass 1 scores, and missing critical elements.

Pass 3 — Reconciler: reviews both prior passes, resolves disagreements, applies question-specific dimension weighting, and produces final scores with confidence levels (HIGH/MEDIUM/LOW). Disagreements above 3 points trigger a review flag visible to hiring managers.

Three sequential Claude API calls per question. Five questions per scenario. Approximately ₹3.75 per assessment. The cost is the moat.

What are anchored rubrics, and why do they matter?

"Anchored" means each score band has a concrete written definition. A 7 in Threat Identification doesn't mean "AI thought it was kind of good" — it means: "Identifies primary and secondary vectors. Accurate MITRE mapping. Explains attacker decision logic."

We publish all 5 dimensions × 5 score bands × 4 difficulty levels — a public scoring rubric anyone can audit. The dimensions are: Threat Identification, Containment & Response, Architecture & Blast Radius, Communication Quality, and Framework Application. Each question weights these dimensions differently — a "map this to MITRE" question weights Threat ID at 40%; an "executive briefing" question weights Communication at 50%.

How do you know your scores are accurate over time?

AI scoring drifts. Anthropic updates models. Prompt tweaks have unintended consequences. Without automated testing, scoring degrades silently — you only discover it when a customer complains.

We run nightly regression testing at 02:00 IST. A library of 50+ golden answers spanning 7 categories — Clearly Excellent, Clearly Poor, Fluent But Wrong, Correct But Poorly Communicated, Overconfident Hallucination, Partial But Well-Prioritized, and Technically Strong But Business-Blind — runs through the live scoring engine. If any answer scores outside its expected range, the engineering team gets a Slack alert immediately. Pass-rate target: 85%+. If it drops below 80%, we freeze prompt changes until investigated.

Can candidates game the assessment with AI?

Copy-paste is disabled on every answer input. Each question is generated by AI based on the previous answer — so pre-prepared responses (or generic AI-generated ones) don't survive the follow-up. Architecture diagrams are drawn from a pool and selected randomly per session. Forward-only navigation prevents revision once a follow-up is seen.

A candidate can certainly use AI to prepare for general interview topics, as they should. What they can't do is copy-paste an answer mid-assessment and get a passing score.
